> A: The principle is very similar to urban planning. Frequently used data is placed close by, and occasionally used data is placed further away. A prime example of a global company specializing in building these communication networks is Broadcom.
>
> Q: To the general public, tech companies might all look similar, but in reality, their unique roles and exclusive technological territories are firmly divided.
>
> A: That's right. The key is to transfer data among HBM, HBF, and GPU as quickly as possible to reduce latency. Currently, data is transmitted as electrical signals through copper wires, but there is a physical limitation where the signal speed drops due to the copper's resistance. To overcome this, the method of transmitting data using 'light' (optical communication) instead of electricity has emerged, which is why silicon photonics-related companies are gaining attention recently. A prime example is the 'InfiniBand' technology acquired by NVIDIA, which handles optical communication between modules. Like the Strait of Hormuz, these monopoly technology companies, holding tight to the bottlenecks in the global chip supply chain, are positioned in their respective fields.

> Q: When do you expect HBF to be commercialized?
>

It is highly likely that one of NVIDIA, AMD, or Google will introduce the first commercialized product. The primary reason Big Tech companies are interested in HBF is to reduce their dependence on Korean companies. If they solely use HBM, they can't escape Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix, but adopting HBF gives them alternatives to collaborate with American companies like SanDisk or Micron.

>
> In 1969, Roger Penrose proposed a mind-bending way to steal energy from a rotating black hole. It sounded like science fiction.
>
> More than 50 years later, researchers at the City University of New York have made a version of this effect real — in a lab, without any actual black hole or physical spinning.
>
> The Breakthrough
> Instead of building a rotating object, the team created a ring-shaped network of resonators whose properties change over time in a carefully controlled way (spatiotemporal modulation). This synthetic “motion” mimics rotation through the Floquet effect, allowing them to observe superradiance — waves coming out with more energy than they went in.
>
> This is the first experimental confirmation of the Zel’dovich effect (an extension of Penrose’s idea) using purely electromagnetic waves in a non-rotating system.
>
> Why It Matters
> It bridges theoretical astrophysics with real-world photonics.
> The experiment opens doors to new types of ultra-efficient amplifiers and wave generators.
> It lets scientists study wave-matter interactions in rotating frames without needing massive, fast-spinning hardware.
>
> Previous experiments used spinning disks or cylinders, but this new approach eliminates mechanical rotation entirely — a cleaner, more elegant demonstration.
>
> While we’re still far from harvesting energy from real black holes, these tabletop analogs help us understand fundamental physics and could lead to revolutionary technologies in wave manipulation and energy harvesting.

> Why temperature matters: Notice the T(temperature) in the equation. > Optical Switches: Instead of charging a capacitor with electricity, a pulse of light switches a material between being transparent or reflective.
> The Power Advantage: Photons (light particles) do not have mass or electrical charge, meaning they do not generate heat through electrical resistance. > Spintronics (Magnetic Bits): Instead of moving electrons from point A to point B, you simply change the spin (magnetic direction) of an electron right where it sits. This uses almost no moving current and has no capacitive electrical loss.
> Phase-Change Memory (PCM): This approach mimics your idea of a physical state change. A tiny electrical pulse switches a glass-like material between an amorphous state (disorganized atoms = 0) and a crystalline state (organized atoms = 1).
