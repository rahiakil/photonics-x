> If you’ve been following AI infrastructure, you’ve probably heard that data centers are hitting what some call the Copper Wall — the point where electrical signals through copper can no longer keep up with the heat, latency, and bandwidth demands of training and running massive AI models. The answer is photonics: moving data as light through fiber instead of electrons through metal.
>
> But photonics isn’t a single product. It’s a deep, layered supply chain stretching from exotic crystal growth in China to fiber trenching across American highways. This guide maps every layer of that chain, names every major public company in it, and explains exactly what each one does.
>
> No data has been omitted. Every company from the original research is here.
>
> Layer 0 — Raw Materials
> What this layer means: This is the floor of the photonics supply chain. Before any chip can be designed, any laser built, or any fiber laid, the raw materials must exist. Indium phosphide (InP), silicon-on-insulator (SOI), gallium arsenide (GaAs), and optical-grade glass fiber are the fundamental outputs of this layer. Every laser, chip, and cable in the chain depends on what gets produced here. It is also the most geopolitically exposed layer in the entire stack: approximately 60–70% of global indium production comes from China.
>
> $AXTI — AXT Inc. Single-crystal InP and GaAs wafer manufacturer. These substrates are the irreplaceable foundation for EML lasers and high-speed photodiodes. NVIDIA’s aggressive push toward 1.6T transceivers has turned InP into a supply bottleneck, and AXTI sits directly at that chokepoint.
>
> $IQE III-V compound semiconductor epitaxy specialist. IQE grows thin-film layers of InP, GaAs, and GaN onto customer wafers, supplying the epitaxial substrate that laser diode and high-speed photodetector manufacturers need. Often called the invisible backbone of the optical module supply chain.
>
> $SOI — Soitec Silicon-on-Insulator wafer manufacturer. SOI wafers are the starting material for silicon photonics foundries. TSMC’s COUPE platform, GlobalFoundries’ Fotonix process, and Tower Semiconductor’s SiPh line all rely on Soitec substrates. When a Broadcom or Marvell photonic chip ships inside a data center, there is a good chance the journey started on a Soitec wafer.
>
> $WOLF — Wolfspeed Silicon carbide (SiC) and gallium nitride (GaN) wafer manufacturer. Not directly tied to photonic lasers, but critical for the high-power electronics and power converters inside data center infrastructure. Wolfspeed feeds the power layer that photonics depends on to function.
>
> $GLW — Corning (also active in Layer 5) The world’s largest manufacturer of optical fiber and specialty glass. Every fiber cable running inside and between data centers starts here. Corning also produces specialty glass components used in transceiver housings and is a named partner in NVIDIA’s Spectrum-X silicon photonics ecosystem. Active in both Layer 0 (raw glass and fiber) and Layer 5 (module components).
>
> $LWLG — Lightwave Logic Polymer-based electro-optic waveguide developer. Rather than silicon, LWLG uses organic polymer materials in the modulators that convert electrical signals to light — promising significantly higher modulation speeds and lower insertion loss. Still in the commercialization phase, making this an early-stage bet on a materials platform that could disrupt the conventional silicon approach.
>
> $SHMN — Shin-Etsu Chemical (also active in Equipment layer) The world’s largest silicon wafer manufacturer. While SiPh foundries use SOI specifically, Shin-Etsu supplies the standard silicon wafer base that underpins the entire semiconductor ecosystem. Also present in the equipment layer as a foundry supply chain essential.
>
> $VECO — Veeco Instruments (also active in Equipment layer) Manufacturer of MBE (Molecular Beam Epitaxy) and MOCVD (Metal-Organic Chemical Vapor Deposition) equipment. Veeco does not make wafers — it makes the machines that grow them. III-V laser wafer production is impossible without MOCVD reactors, which is where Veeco’s role sits. Active in both Layer 0 (enabling raw material production) and the Equipment layer.
