> The bottleneck framing is right, but the sequence being described here stops one layer short of where the pressure actually lands in high-stakes deployment contexts like healthcare.
>
> Compute, then interconnect, then photonics, yes. But the binding constraint for clinical AI isn't just moving data between GPUs faster. It's the energy cost of inference at the point where the economics have to close against reimbursement rates. Nvidia's GB200 delivers roughly 30 times better performance per watt on certain inference tasks versus the H100 (their own published benchmarks). That number matters more in a clinical workflow than raw throughput, because you're not optimizing for speed, you're optimizing for cost-per-query against what Medicare will actually pay.
>
> Photonics solves a real problem for hyperscale data centers routing between racks. It's a meaningful unlock for training clusters. But the constraint that actually determines whether a community hospital can run continuous real-time ICU monitoring (processing vitals, imaging, lab values, and unstructured notes simultaneously across a whole floor) is compute-per-watt at the edge, not optical interconnect bandwidth inside a data center someone else operates.
>
> The pattern this post describes is accurate. Where it gets complicated is assuming the next constraint is singular. Interconnect matters for centralized inference at scale. Energy efficiency matters for distributed inference at the care delivery layer. Those aren't the same problem, and the companies solving one won't automatically solve the other.
>
> The X-ray analogy is useful here: the constraint wasn't generating electricity, it was delivering reliable current to a specific room in a specific building (which took decades longer than generating it centrally).

>
> A modern GPU has thousands of cores processing data in parallel. 
> Inside a GPU or between nearby GPUs, data is increasingly traveling through light (photonics). 
>
> But between racks (a few meters apart), copper is still dominant. 
>
> Copper consumes enormous energy and generates massive heat. 
>
> At the scale of clusters with tens of thousands of GPUs, this becomes a critical bottleneck.
>
> This is where photonics — CPO (Co-Packaged Optics) for the intra GPU data transmission and LRO for the inter rack data transmission — becomes essential: replacing copper with light dramatically reduces energy per bit and frees up power for actual computation instead of cooling.
>
> The three main laser players in this revolution:
>
> •  Lumentum (LITE) and Coherent (COHR): Leaders in high-power lasers (EML). Their strength is raw power, ideal for longer distances and inter-rack connections.
>
> •  Sivers: Its strength lies in low-power multi-λ CW laser arrays (multiple wavelengths on a single chip). These lasers are particularly suited for highly integrated, ultra-energy-efficient architectures needed for intra-GPU or near-GPU connections.
>
> The TAM is becoming massive.
>
> As Marvell highlighted in a recent official post, the co-packaged connectivity market (CPO + NPO) is expected to grow from under 1 million ports in 2025 to over 100 million ports per year by 2030. That’s explosive growth !! 
>

>

>
> •  Lumentum and Coherent will take a very large share of inter-rack connections (longer distances, massive volumes).

> I've been looking into ZBLAN optical fiber as it's one of the clearer cases for manufacturing in space. 
>
> ZBLAN is a heavy metal fluoride glass fiber. It offers the potential for lower optical loss than conventional silica fiber and transmits across a broader wavelength range into the mid-infrared. These traits could support longer transmission distances and new uses in sensing and communications.
>
> On Earth, producing high-quality ZBLAN has been difficult. Gravity induces convection in the molten glass during the drawing process leads to microscopic crystals and defects that scatter light and limit performance.
>
> In microgravity, these issues are reduced. The glass stays more homogeneous during drawing. Recent work by Flawless Photonics on the International Space Station produced over 11 kilometers of fiber in about a month.
>
> The returned samples are under analysis, and microscope comparisons show much cleaner microstructures than Earth-produced fiber.
>
> Challenges remain. ZBLAN is more brittle than silica and has a larger minimum bend radius, which affects handling. The technology is still in the demonstration phase but commercial production is being planned,and the plan is to manufacture it in space.

> With all this talk about connectivity $MRVL $AVGO $CRDO … 
>
> I did a deep dive into the 1.6 T optical DSP… 
>
> Ultimately the most underappreciated thing about $CRDO optical story isn’t the DSP. 
>
> It’s that they now own nearly every block of a finished 1.6T module — SerDes, optical DSP (Bluebird/Cardinal), silicon photonics and laser drivers (DustPhotonics), the ZeroFlap transceiver assembly, and the PILOT reliability layer on top. 
>
> $MRVL & $AVGO sell the brains — the DSP — into an ecosystem where a separate module maker sources lasers from one vendor and photonics from another and bolts it together. $CRDO is the single name on the whole thing.
>
> Why that matters compounds four ways. 
>
> 1. ASP capture, and this one’s concrete: a discrete DSP or PIC carries two-digit ASPs, a complete ZeroFlap module carries three-digit ASPs. That’s why ZeroFlap is the single largest contributor to the >$600M FY27 optical guide despite being one of three lines. Integration converts straight into dollars per link.
>
> 2. co-design. When one team owns the DSP, photonics, and laser drivers, it tunes the interfaces together instead of designing to a spec and praying three vendors’ parts play nice. That’s where the “fewer lasers” architecture comes from — lower power, fewer failure points, less exposure to laser supply bottlenecks. A vendor stitching third-party components can’t optimize across boundaries it doesn’t control.
>
> 3. it sidesteps the fight Credo would lose. As a merchant DSP they’re the third entrant against Marvell (in volume) and Broadcom (entrenched), in a market where design wins are sticky because re-qualifying silicon is slow. Selling a whole module changes the customer’s question from “is Credo’s DSP better than Marvell’s” to “is this complete module better for my power and uptime constraints.” That’s a contest Credo can win on architecture instead of incumbency.
>
> 4. supply-chain control. With optical supply tight, owning more of the BOM in-house means fewer external dependencies gating shipments — and fewer lasers compounds it.
>
> This is a focused-niche edge, not a moat against the giants’ scale. $CRDO wins where power and reliability matter more than raw volume — in-rack and scale-up fabric links. It doesn’t make them bigger or more manufacturing-deep than $MRVL or $AVGO, who can still out-supply them on commodity volume. 
>
> The bet is owning a defensible, high-ASP slice — the module — rather than competing as one component among many. 
>
> The >$600M optical guide and the ZeroFlap-led mix are the validation, not the spec sheet.
