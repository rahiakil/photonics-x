> $IONQ

> A recent report by Kearney makes it clear that trapped ions are the most promising way of building commercially useful quantum computers.
>
> The report discusses these architectures:
>
> 1/ Trapped ions
>
> Highest fidelity but slower gate speeds
>
> All-to-all connectivity leads to lower physical qubit overhead
>
> Best from unit-economics perspective
>
> Bottom line: It’s the most commercially viable quantum computers. You’ll get high quality data when using them but have to wait for your calculations longer.
>
> 2/ Superconducting qubits
>
> Fast gate times
>
> Higher physical qubit overhead than for trapped ions
>
> But even under optimistic assumptions, scaling will make system costs unsustainable
>
> Report actually calls this an “existential architectural threat”
>
> Bottom line: There’s a good chance you’ll never see a useful quantum computer built on millions of superconducting qubits
>
> 3/ Neutral atoms
>
> High fidelity
>
> All-to-all connectivity leading to lower physical qubit overhead like for trapped ions
>
> As promising as trapped-ion technology but real challenge is engineering: Calibration automation, system resets, and error correction
>
> Bottom line: Definitely has potential to scale up but not clear how engineering challenges will be overcome
>
> 4/ Photonics
>
> Combines two advantages: Room-temperature operation and compatibility with foundries currently building semiconductor chips
>
> Biggest bet is modularity, which means you break quantum computers into small interconnected processing units
>
> Biggest challenge is scaling because photons don’t naturally interact with each other and light is subject to losses
>
> Bottom line: Not clear if this manufacturing leverage will help scale photonics.
>
> The conclusion of the report speaks for itself:
>
> “The architectures most likely to lead are therefore the ones that can repeatedly translate physics progress into operationally stable, cost-improving systems, because the market will ultimately reward not “qubits on a slide,” but sustained improvement in error-corrected capability per dollar.
>
> Based on Kearney analysis in collaboration with leading quantum companies, industry and academic experts, trapped-ion quantum computers are early leaders to be the most cost-effective quantum computer at fault tolerant scale.”

> $COHR $LITE $AAOI
>
> Memory wall today, photonics wall tomorrow? 
>
> Currently, HBM memory is the loudest constraint in the AI supply chain. AI dedicated supply is concentrated in 3 players (SK hynix, Samsung, and Micron), sold out on key nodes, and accounts for ~40% of AI server BOM. Per-GPU capacity is climbing but model sizes, context lengths, and inference demands are growing much faster. This is the memory wall we are witnessing now. 
>
> But interconnects are quietly becoming the utilization killer. Even w/ plenty of GPUs and HBM, poor all-reduce / collective performance across racks and pods tanks effective FLOPS. Copper hits hard walls on power, heat, signal integrity, and reach as densities explode. Networking already eats rising % of total power and can drop GPU utilization dramatically w/ even minor packet loss or congestion.
>
> Photonics / optical interconnects (CPO, silicon photonics, optical I/O) are the only credible path to break the electrical interconnect ceiling. They deliver higher bandwidth density, lower power/bit at distance, and enable true scale-up domains beyond what copper or even current NVLink can do. Hyperscalers and Nvidia are already accelerating adoption. 
>
> Here’s the twist: optics themselves are now hitting manufacturing and supply bottlenecks. There are scaling challenges in silicon photonics capacity, testing/qualification cycles, and especially upstream lasers (EMLs undershipping demand) w/ Nvidia making strategic investments ahead of time. Integration, fiber attach, and high volume heterogeneous packaging are harder and slower to ramp than HBM production lines. HBM has a multi-year roadmap and dedicated capacity fights. Photonics is earlier in its high-volume AI curve w/ longer qualification, lower initial yields, and more complex supply chain (lasers + PICs + DSPs).
>
> During this buildout, I wouldn’t be shocked if the tighter constraint flips at times:
> •  Near-term: Memory remains painful (especially for training large models).
> •  Mid-term (as clusters get massive and power efficiency becomes non-negotiable): Photonics and advanced optical networking could gate deployment and utilization more severely. You can’t just “add more HBM” if the fabric connecting everything can’t keep up w/o killing power budgets or latency.
>
> Power is the ultimate governor underneath both but optics are both part of the solution and a new constraint layer while the ecosystem scales.
>
> Winners will be those who solve the optical supply chain (lasers, SiPh foundries, test/assembly specialists) or architectures that minimize data movement in the first place. The AI buildout isn’t just “more GPUs.” It is a co-evolution of memory, interconnects, power, and packaging. The binding constraint continues. 
>
> Optical (photonics) interconnects are expected to be the next major theme in addressing bottlenecks during the AI infrastructure buildout. Although photonics-related stocks have been getting hammered lately, the sector’s growth cycle ironically remains in its relatively early stages, even after the initial huge run-up and subsequent corrections. Take advantage of the opportunities accordingly.

> From Grok: Microgravity (and to a lesser extent, the vacuum and radiation environment of space) enables manufacturing processes that are hindered on Earth by gravity-induced effects like sedimentation, buoyancy-driven convection, and structural deformation. This leads to more uniform, defect-free, or otherwise superior materials that can be worth the high cost of launch and return for high-value, low-mass products.31
> Here are the main categories of things that can be best manufactured (higher quality/performance) or, in some cases, only practically manufactured in space:
> 1. High-Quality Protein and Small-Molecule Crystals for Pharmaceuticals
> •Why space? In microgravity, there’s no sedimentation or convection currents, so crystals grow more slowly and uniformly, forming larger, more ordered structures with fewer defects. This improves drug formulation, delivery (e.g., better-controlled release particles), stability, and enables structure determination via X-ray crystallography for new drug design.35
> •Examples: Protein crystals (e.g., for Merck’s Keytruda), small-molecule drugs like ritonavir (HIV medication). Companies like Varda Space have flown missions producing these.4
> •Status: One of the most commercially promising near-term areas due to high value-per-gram.
> 2. Advanced Optical Fibers (Especially ZBLAN)
> •Why space? On Earth, gravity causes crystallization defects and imperfections during fiber drawing from molten glass. Microgravity suppresses these, yielding fibers with far lower signal loss (potentially orders of magnitude better).32
> •Example: ZBLAN (zirconium-barium-lanthanum-sodium-aluminum fluoride glass) fibers for infrared transmission, lasers, medical imaging, and high-speed communications. Companies like Flawless Photonics have produced kilometers of it on the ISS.49
> •Potential: Could revolutionize telecom, sensing, and medical applications.
> 3. Semiconductors, Electronic Crystals, and Related Materials
> •Why space? Reduced convection and sedimentation allow more homogeneous, defect-free crystal growth and layered structures.
> •Examples: Semiconductor wafers, scintillator crystals for radiation detection, improved thin films, and electrode materials. Also, potential for better solar cells or quantum materials.35
> 4. Uniform Alloys, Metals, and Foams
> •Why space? Diffusion dominates over gravity-driven separation/sedimentation, leading to more uniform microstructures. Containerless processing (levitation) is easier. Metal foams can form without collapsing.37
> •Examples: Specialized alloys with superior properties; metal foams for lightweight, high-strength applications (though some foams are made on Earth, microgravity can enable better uniformity).11
> 5. Biological and Medical Products (Tissues, Cells, Devices)
> •Why space? No gravity-induced slumping or uneven layering in 3D structures; better nutrient diffusion and cell organization.
> •Examples: Artificial retinas, engineered tissues, pluripotent stem cells, bio-inks for bioprinting, and drug-delivery devices. Microgravity helps mature cardiac cells or create delicate structures.34
> 6. Precision Spheres and Micro-Encapsulation
> •Why space? Surface tension naturally forms perfect spheres in zero-g without gravitational deformation.
> •Examples: Monodisperse latex spheres (used as calibration standards; early commercial space product), perfect ball bearings, hollow spheres, or microcapsules for drugs.39
> 7. Other Advanced Materials and Processes
> •Specialized glass and ceramics: More uniform or complex shapes without slumping (e.g., via additive manufacturing).20
> •Containerless processing: High-purity materials melted and solidified without crucible contamination.

### TheValueist (@TheValueist)
_Thu Jul 02 22:02:29 +0000 2026_ · ♥ 53 · ↻ 5 · 💬 6 · [open on X](https://x.com/TheValueist/status/2072803120984297485)

> $AXTI $COHR Do you believe this is last InP prepayment deal that will be cut over the next 5 years? We merely getting started.
> ———————-
> BOTTOM LINE: This is incrementally bullish for COHR fundamentals but more strategically important than financially material. It confirms that 6-inch InP substrate supply remains a gating item for COHR’s AI optical ramp, validates AXT/Tongmei as a meaningful 6-inch InP substrate supplier, and shows COHR is using customer-style capacity prepayments upstream to lock supply through the next 3 years. It should be more stock-moving for AXTI than COHR, but for COHR it is a positive de-risking datapoint after a weak tape day, not a thesis-changing event by itself.
>
> The agreement is straightforward but important. AXT-Tongmei entered a 3-year Master Development and Supply Agreement with Coherent, effective June 25, 2026, covering “mass development and supply” of 6-inch indium phosphide wafer substrates. AXT will expand capacity at its Beijing facility from 2026 through 2028, Coherent will make a $22.2885M prepayment for committed capacity, wafer prices are pre-established, and the prepayment will be applied against future product purchases. Coherent also gets refund rights if unused amounts remain at expiry or termination, and can terminate and recover unused prepayment if AXT fails to meet the capacity commitment for more than 6 successive months. AXT gets protection if Coherent misses minimum order quantities, in which case unused prepayment becomes nonrefundable and AXT can terminate. AXT must also offer additional capacity beyond the committed quantity to Coherent on the same terms. The full agreement will not be available until AXT files its Q2 2026 10-Q.  
>
> The key interpretation is that this is not about outsourcing COHR’s differentiated InP device manufacturing. AXT is supplying 6-inch InP wafer substrates, the upstream crystalline substrate input. Coherent still does the value-added photonic device work: EMLs, CW lasers, photodiodes, PICs and related integration. That distinction matters because COHR’s moat remains process/yield/device manufacturing and vertical integration; AXT is enabling more wafer starts, not replacing COHR’s 6-inch InP fab advantage.
>
> For COHR, the positive read is supply-chain de-risking. Management has repeatedly framed InP capacity as the key constraint in the AI optical cycle, while also saying 6-inch InP is central to the revenue and gross margin ramp. In the May earnings call, COHR said its 6-inch platform was producing EMLs, CW lasers and photodiodes, with yields exceeding 3-inch lines, first 6-inch-derived transceiver shipments already contributing to revenue and margin, and internal InP capacity expected to double ahead of plan and more than double again by the end of calendar 2027.   This AXT agreement strengthens confidence that substrate availability will not become the hidden bottleneck that prevents those internal wafer starts from scaling.
>
> This also lines up with the J.P. Morgan investor-meeting takeaway that COHR had substrate supply “locked in” across 5 suppliers spanning multiple geographies, several capable of producing 6-inch substrates. The AXT filing effectively names 1 of those upstream supply arrangements and shows there is real money, fixed pricing, and committed capacity behind the company’s prior substrate comments.  
>
> The less bullish read is that the deal confirms the bottleneck is real and persistent. Coherent would not be making take-or-pay-like commitments and prepaying a supplier for 3-year capacity if 6-inch InP substrates were abundant. Reuters recently reported that China’s InP export controls have disrupted the AI photonics supply chain, that AXT and Sumitomo dominate global InP substrate production, that AXT is a major supplier to Coherent, and that 6-inch InP wafer prices have reportedly risen sharply since China tightened export licensing.   The agreement helps COHR, but it does not eliminate geopolitical risk because the capacity expansion is at AXT’s Beijing facility, precisely the jurisdiction where export licensing and materials controls have become a live issue.
>
> The deal is financially immaterial to COHR in isolation. COHR’s market cap is roughly $65B and the $22.3M prepayment is de minimis against the equity value, capex program, and cash balance. It is best viewed as a capacity reservation / working-capital deposit, likely recorded as a prepaid asset and then consumed through substrate purchases, not as an immediate P&L event. COHR was trading near $333 after a roughly -9.6% move on July 2, so the stock reaction is more likely to be driven by AI hardware sentiment, valuation, and semiconductor tape than by a $22M supplier prepayment.
>
> For AXTI, however, the signal is materially stronger. AXT reported Q1 2026 revenue of $26.9M, so a $22.3M prepayment is roughly 83% of a single quarter’s reported revenue, even though it is not immediately recognized as revenue and the total contract value could be meaningfully different from the prepayment. AXT also said in April that it had completed a $632.5M capital raise to support Tongmei’s InP capacity expansion and 6-inch InP R&D, so the COHR contract provides named strategic-customer validation for that capital deployment.   AXTI was down roughly -12.9% on July 2, and this after-close filing could support a rebound when regular trading resumes.
>
> The most important subtlety is that the $22.3M should not be treated as the total revenue opportunity. The filing says the prepayment is applied against purchases and ties to committed capacity; it does not disclose wafer volumes, per-wafer prices, shipment cadence, gross margin, or whether additional capacity is expected to be purchased beyond the initial commitment. Using Reuters’ cited $5,000 average 6-inch InP wafer price as a purely illustrative benchmark, the prepayment would equate to roughly 4,500 wafers, but that calculation should not be used as a volume estimate because actual contract pricing is confidential and the prepayment may only represent a portion of the broader commitment.  
>
> The fixed-price element is meaningful. If spot 6-inch InP pricing continues to rise, Coherent has partially hedged substrate cost and capacity access. That matters for the gross margin bridge because COHR’s >42% non-GAAP gross margin path relies on 6-inch cost/yield benefits, higher 1.6T mix, internal sourcing, and better absorption. J.P. Morgan’s May conference note highlighted 42%+ gross margin tailwinds from the shift from 800G to higher-margin 1.6T, the transition from 3-inch to 6-inch wafers, and movement from external to internal sourcing.   The AXT agreement supports that framework, but the benefit likely shows up gradually through wafer starts, device output, module shipments, and margin conversion rather than immediately in the next quarter.
>
> This is also a useful read-through to AI optical demand. Coherent is not just receiving long-term customer commitments; it is passing those commitments upstream. That is how a real supply-constrained hardware cycle behaves. Customers commit to COHR, COHR commits to AXT, AXT expands Beijing 6-inch capacity, and the entire chain becomes increasingly governed by deposits, capacity reservations, fixed prices, minimum orders, and termination protections. This reinforces the view that the AI optical cycle is capacity constrained rather than purely speculative order inflation, though it does not disprove some double-ordering risk.
>
> The agreement also has a China-risk wrinkle that investors should not ignore. Coherent has emphasized U.S. manufacturing scale, Sherman 6-inch InP leadership, CHIPS-related support, and the NVIDIA strategic partnership. This AXT agreement reveals that even a U.S.-anchored 6-inch InP device strategy still depends on upstream substrate inputs from China. That is not inherently negative, because AXT is a scaled and strategically important substrate supplier, but it means export permits, U.S.-China policy, force majeure language, and qualification of non-China supply remain critical diligence items.
>
> Relative implications are clear. For COHR, this is a positive data point for execution confidence and supply visibility, but not enough on its own to revise estimates. For AXTI, it is a much bigger validation event because it confirms that a leading AI optical platform company is willing to prepay for Tongmei 6-inch substrate capacity. For LITE, the read-through is mixed: positive for the industry because InP scarcity is validated, but potentially negative at the margin if COHR is locking a larger share of scarce AXT 6-inch output. For the broader optical ecosystem, the deal supports the view that the scarcity value sits not only in finished transceivers but also deep upstream in compound semiconductor substrates, lasers, isolators, PM fiber, ELS modules, and other enabling components.
>

The better near-term setup is probably AXTI up more sharply on customer validation and COHR modestly supported, with the caveat that U.S. markets are closed Friday, July 3 for Independence Day observed, so regular-session price discovery is delayed.   COHR remains a high-conviction structural AI optical beneficiary, but the stock still needs gross margin progression, 6-inch output conversion, and FCF inflection to justify the valuation. This announcement improves confidence in the supply-chain bridge to that outcome.
>
> Key diligence questions for Monday/next week: whether this AXT capacity was already included in COHR’s “5 substrate suppliers” commentary or is incremental; whether export licenses are already secured; what share of FY2027 6-inch wafer starts this covers; whether the agreement includes first-priority capacity rights or merely committed capacity; whether the fixed wafer prices are below current spot; how quickly AXT can qualify volume 6-inch wafers to COHR specifications; and whether any of the prepayment is ultimately customer-funded through COHR’s own strategic LTAs.
