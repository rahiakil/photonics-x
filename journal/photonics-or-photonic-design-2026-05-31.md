> The bottleneck framing is right, but the sequence being described here stops one layer short of where the pressure actually lands in high-stakes deployment contexts like healthcare.
>
> Compute, then interconnect, then photonics, yes. But the binding constraint for clinical AI isn't just moving data between GPUs faster. It's the energy cost of inference at the point where the economics have to close against reimbursement rates. Nvidia's GB200 delivers roughly 30 times better performance per watt on certain inference tasks versus the H100 (their own published benchmarks). That number matters more in a clinical workflow than raw throughput, because you're not optimizing for speed, you're optimizing for cost-per-query against what Medicare will actually pay.
>
> Photonics solves a real problem for hyperscale data centers routing between racks. It's a meaningful unlock for training clusters. But the constraint that actually determines whether a community hospital can run continuous real-time ICU monitoring (processing vitals, imaging, lab values, and unstructured notes simultaneously across a whole floor) is compute-per-watt at the edge, not optical interconnect bandwidth inside a data center someone else operates.
>
> The pattern this post describes is accurate. Where it gets complicated is assuming the next constraint is singular. Interconnect matters for centralized inference at scale. Energy efficiency matters for distributed inference at the care delivery layer. Those aren't the same problem, and the companies solving one won't automatically solve the other.
>
> The X-ray analogy is useful here: the constraint wasn't generating electricity, it was delivering reliable current to a specific room in a specific building (which took decades longer than generating it centrally).

> I see a lot of people saying Sivers is not a bottleneck so I wanted to write a quick thread on what actually makes something a bottleneck in photonics — and why $SIVE fits, even if many people say it doesn’t.
>
> A lot of people get confused and think bottleneck is just “being the only supplier.” instead, It’s when a specific component is difficult and slow to replace at scale, because of technical complexity, long qualification times, or limited alternatives that meet the required performance.
>
> Many people look at SIVE and say “it’s not a bottleneck” because:
> - It doesn’t own its own high-volume fab
> - There are other laser companies (Lumentum $LITE , Coherent $COHR , etc.)
> - Hyperscalers usually multi-source
>
> However, that misses the point:
> SIVE’s position is narrow but very meaningful because very few suppliers can deliver the specific combination of power, wavelength stability, and thermal performance that several CPO architectures require.
>
> Once these lasers are qualified into a system like $SIVE is with  (Ayar, Jabil ecosystem, $POET, etc.), switching is slow and risky. Qualification cycles in this layer are long. That friction is what creates a bottleneck — even without a full monopoly.
