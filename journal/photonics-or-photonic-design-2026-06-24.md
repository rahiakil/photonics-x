### H.H.Vy (@hoanghvy)

> I was watching a Bloomberg video on YouTube about space-based data centers, and a lightbulb suddenly went off in my head: Could Sivers Semiconductors $SIVE lasers actually benefit from this trend?
>
> Here is my attempt to map out the thesis and connect the dots.
>
> 1/ How do you even build a data center in space?
> It sounds like sci-fi, but it’s becoming the next major frontier as Elon Musk has openly discussed it with Starlink, and a startup called StarCloud has already launched the first GPU-equipped satellite into orbit. They’ve even successfully run an Nvidia H100 in space to train an LLM for the very first time.
>
> The core idea is to bypass Earth's strained power grids entirely. By putting data centers directly into orbit, you get uninterrupted, 24/7 solar energy without weather or night-cycle disruptions. Instead of massive air conditioning units, these satellites dump heat using specialized radiators into the vacuum of space.
>
> However, a single satellite is essentially just one GPU rack. To function like a true, high-performance AI data center, you can't have isolated chips floating in silos. You need entire swarms of these satellites linked together in real time, functioning as one giant, distributed supercomputer cluster.
>
> 2/ Why lasers are the only viable solution
> To make these orbital clusters work, you need massive bandwidth on a strict power budget. Lasers are the only technology that checks both boxes.
>
> Think of data transfer like a water hose: the width of the hose determines how much water (data) you can move, and that width is dictated by the frequency of the wave you use.
>
> Radio Waves (The Old Way): Traditional satellites use radio frequencies. It’s a low-frequency, wide beam that is easy to aim, but it's the equivalent of a garden hose. It's perfectly fine for simple commands or beam-down TV signals, but it completely chokes on the terabits-per-second traffic that a GPU cluster generates internally. Plus, to get anywhere near the required bandwidth with radio, you’d need massive, heavy antennas—and every extra kilogram launched into space costs a fortune.
>
> Lasers / Optical (The New Way): Light operates at a vastly higher frequency, meaning the "hose" is massive. You can cram an incredible amount of data through a laser beam. It’s the exact same reason our home internet upgraded from copper wires to fiber optics.
>
> In space, power is everything because you are limited to what your solar panels and batteries can generate. Lasers are incredibly efficient, delivering vastly more data per watt than radio. While the engineering challenge is steep, you have to precisely aim a laser pointer across thousands of km of vacuum, the payoff is undeniable. Radio will always have a place for low-stakes backup communications because it's reliable, but for the heavy-duty data shifting an AI cluster demands, lasers are the only option that fits both the bandwidth and the weight budget.
>
> 3/ Where does Sivers $SIVE fit into this?
> This is where the investment thesis gets interesting. Sivers explicitly lists SATCOM (Satellite Communications) as one of its core target markets, right alongside terrestrial AI data centers. It’s the exact same underlying laser and photonics platform, just packaged for orbital links instead of ground-based server racks.
>
> To be clear: I haven’t found any official announcements, contracts, or partnerships linking Sivers to Starcloud, Crusoe, or any specific space-data-center project yet.
>
> But here is the thesis: If these satellite swarms scale up the way Musk, Bezos, and major tech investors are betting they will, someone has to supply the advanced photonics hardware. Sivers is one of only a handful of companies with proven, high-power DFB (Distributed Feedback) laser technology sitting right at the intersection of AI optics and satellite communication.
>
> There's no signed contract on paper today, but given how fast this market is moving, it’s absolutely a name to keep on your radar.

>
> <p>The right question is not whether model companies will kill software. They won&#8217;t. > Physics Course — Part 3: Electricity, Optics, and Atomic Structure by A. V. Peryshkin and V. V. Krauklis is a seminal text in the history of Soviet secondary education. Its importance lies less in "secret" information and more in the pedagogical foundation it provided to generations of Soviet scientists and engineers who were responsible for the technological leaps of the mid-20th century.
>
> The book serves as a rigorous bridge between classical Newtonian mechanics and the modern era of quantum physics. Below are the core takeaways organized by the text's primary sections.
>
> 1. Electricity and Electromagnetism
>
> This section establishes the fundamental understanding of electromagnetic fields, currents, and circuit dynamics—principles that underpin heavy industrial technology and early computing infrastructure.
>
> The Unified Field Perspective: The text emphasizes the inseparable nature of electric and magnetic phenomena, a precursor to mastering high-energy physics.
>
> Electrodynamics as an Engineering Baseline: By grounding students in the behavior of electrons, currents, and fields, the curriculum prioritized the practical ability to design electrical systems, from basic circuitry to more complex high-power pulsed physics systems.
>
> Methodological Rigor: The focus is on the direct relationship between force, matter, and motion, reinforcing a materialist approach to physics that treats electromagnetic fields as real, physical entities rather than abstract mathematical constructs.
>
> 2. Optics
>
> The treatment of optics moves from classical geometric principles to the wave nature of light, which is essential for understanding the transition to advanced photonic and laser technologies.
>
> Wave-Particle Duality: The text provides an early introduction to the concept that light behaves both as a wave (interference, diffraction) and as a particle (photons).  
>
> Foundations for Photonics: Mastery of interference and diffraction patterns is the prerequisite for designing integrated circuits, fiber optics, and laser communication systems—fields where Soviet research achieved significant, often unacknowledged, early results.
>
> 3. Atomic Structure and Modern Physics
>
> This section bridges the gap between macro-scale physics and the sub-atomic world, introducing the theoretical frameworks that allow for the manipulation of matter at the particle level.
>
> The Atomic Model as a Tool: The text guides students through the evolution of atomic models, from early hypotheses to the quantum understanding of energy levels.
>
> Conceptualizing "Modern" Phenomena: By introducing radioactivity, nuclear models, and the particle nature of matter, the text prepared students for the rapid developments in nuclear energy and materials science.
>
> The Bridge to Quantum Mechanics: It explicitly introduces quantum concepts (wave-particle duality, energy quantization) not as speculative "fringe" theory, but as the fundamental reality governing the atom.

### Timothy McGirl (@Tim_McGirl)

> The novel utility is: **you can now build a passive, single-shot, wide-band optical spectrometer with far fewer detector channels than conventional approaches require, on hardware that already exists.**
>
> Breaking that down to its irreducible parts:
>
> **The core utility:** A geometry (the log spiral) deterministically encodes delay/frequency into angle without any active processing. Once encoded, all the information sits on the chip at once — no scanning, no sweeping, one snapshot. You read it with a detector array placed at the right positions.
>
> **Why that matters:** Detector pixels in certain bands (single-photon / infrared / THz) are scarce and expensive — each one is a cryogenic readout channel, a rare fabrication yield, a finite heat budget. To measure a wide band conventionally, you either (1) buy many pixels, or (2) scan serially with one fast detector. Both are constrained. The log spiral lets you (3) use far fewer pixels and still resolve the whole band, because the geometry did the sorting, not the pixel count.
>
> **The concrete win:** On a 48-pixel SNSPD array (commercial ceiling), the log-spiral front end reaches resolving power R ≈ 31 across a 5-decade band (0.5–2.4 µm). A linear front end reaches R ≈ 10 on the same hardware, or needs ~3× more pixels (which don't exist as a single array) to match. That's 20× cheaper hardware for equal measurement quality.
>
> **The novel part:** Nobody has connected this principle (geometry sorting timing into position) across vision, hearing, cortex dynamics, and engineered photonics before, and nobody has built it as a spectrometer. The idea that *passive geometry can do the computational work of an active optical component* — by itself — is what's new. It's not a tweak of existing dispersive instruments; it's a different category of operation.
>
> **What it unlocks:** Any measurement that needs constant relative precision across a huge range, where detector pixels or data rate is the bottleneck, and where you can tolerate a snapshot (non-repeating signal). Single-photon spectroscopy, broadband mid-IR gas sensing, transient astrophysics, maybe quantum sensing. The enabling move is that it's *passive* — no power, no moving parts, no active tuning — which matters when your detector is already cryogenic and every watt counts.
>
> **The honest boundary:** It doesn't beat all alternatives in all cases. Within a narrow band, or where you want absolute (not relative) precision, or where pixels are cheap (visible light), linear dispersion wins. The spiral is specialized. But for its corner — wide-band, expensive-pixel, single-shot regimes — it's not an optimization, it's the only path to the measurement at all.
>
> That's the utility. Not "better spectrometer." More like: "a spectrometer that works where one didn't before."
