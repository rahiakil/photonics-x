> CMOS manufacturing and silicon photonics manufacturing operate at very different physical scales — and that changes the process-control conversation.
> In advanced CMOS, critical dielectric features can be on the order of ~1.5–2 nm. There sub-nm variation can impact device performance. Typically in the form of threshold-voltage shift, which may still be electrically compensated in some cases.
> In silicon photonics, waveguide features are much larger, typically 100s of  nm. Relative to Si electronics, a few nanometers of process tolerance in SiPho can still translate to tighter relative control, often below 1%.
> Furthermore, the variation can impact optical performance, and unlike electronics, there may be limited or no electrical compensation available.
> That is the key manufacturing tradeoff:
> 100× larger features.
> 10× tighter relative control.
> Unless you opt for post-fabrication correction.
> As silicon photonics continues to scale into high-volume applications, mainly for optical interconnects, AI infrastructure, and co-packaged optics, manufacturing control will be just as critical as device design.
> The challenge is not simply making smaller features.
> It is making optical features predictable, repeatable, and manufacturable at scale.
