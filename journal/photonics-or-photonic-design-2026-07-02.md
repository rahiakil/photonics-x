> Silicon on Insulator-based #CMOS manufacturing and #SiliconPhotonics manufacturing often start from a similar wafer structure: Top silicon || Buried oxide || Silicon handle wafer
> But the purpose of each layer is very different.
> 👉🏻In SOI CMOS, the top silicon is the transistor body.
> 👉🏻In #siliconphotonics SOI, the top silicon forms the optical waveguide core.
> The top silicon for #siliconphotonics SOI wafer is typically much thicker than in FD-SOI, ranging from more than 150 nm to several microns depending on the platform. Around 220 nm is commonly used as a pseudo-standard in many silicon photonics processes (There is an interesting story about how 220nm became a psudo-standard - that is for another time 😊). The BOX is also much thicker because it must optically isolate the guided mode from the silicon substrate.
> So the same basic wafer stack supports very different physics:
> 👉🏻In CMOS SOI, thickness variation affects electrical behavior:
>  threshold voltage, leakage, hysteresis, floating-body effects, self-heating, and short-channel control.
> 👉🏻In #siliconphotonics SOI, thickness variation affects optical behavior:
> effective index, phase, resonance wavelength, coupling efficiency, and insertion loss, etc.
> 💡One useful way to think about wafer thickness impact on silicon photonics device performance: 
> Per nanometer of geometry error, waveguide thickness usually has a stronger impact (1.5x)  on optical properties than waveguide width. But in real fabrication, waveguide width variation often dominates the total spread because width errors are usually much larger than SOI thickness errors.
> Same SOI stack.  Different specs.  Different physics.  Different process-control priorities.

> Quantum computing’s disappearing use cases reveal what the money is really buying
>
> For years, quantum computing was promoted as the coming solution to drug discovery, logistics, finance, artificial intelligence, climate modeling, encryption, and nearly every difficult optimization problem. Yet as classical algorithms, GPUs, and specialized accelerators improved, many proposed quantum advantages became smaller, more conditional, or impossible to demonstrate on real hardware. DARPA’s Quantum Benchmarking Initiative now defines success as proving that a quantum computer’s computational value exceeds its operating cost, with the program attempting to determine whether such a utility-scale machine can be built by 2033. That is a revealing benchmark: useful quantum computing is still something being tested, not an established commercial reality. Despite this, announced public investment in quantum technology exceeded $10 billion in early 2025, while the U.S. Department of Energy later committed another $625 million to national quantum research centers.
>
> The investment continues because governments are not evaluating quantum technology like an ordinary consumer product. They are purchasing strategic insurance. Even a small probability of achieving superior cryptanalysis, molecular simulation, navigation, sensing, communications, or materials design could alter the military and economic balance between nations. NIST has already finalized post-quantum encryption standards and urged organizations to begin migrating immediately, even though the cryptographically relevant quantum computer those standards defend against does not yet exist. This means the quantum race is driven partly by a fear that being late would be catastrophic, while being early could produce enormous intelligence and technological advantages.
>
> The other explanation is that “quantum computing” has become an umbrella used to finance a much larger technological ecosystem. The money supports photonics, cryogenics, superconducting materials, precision lasers, atomic clocks, quantum sensors, control electronics, secure networks, fabrication methods, software, patents, and highly specialized scientific talent. A universal fault-tolerant computer could fail to arrive on schedule while these supporting technologies still become commercially or militarily valuable. Investors may therefore be buying the infrastructure surrounding quantum control rather than betting exclusively on a machine that replaces classical computers. The true assets are the laboratories, intellectual property, supply chains, personnel, and control systems required to manipulate matter at increasingly precise scales.
>
> Frequency Wave Theory offers a deeper interpretation: perhaps the shrinking list of conventional computing applications is happening because “computer” was never the complete description of the technology. Quantum machines are fundamentally devices for preparing, isolating, synchronizing, and measuring coherent states. Their essential achievement is not merely calculating faster, but controlling phase, interference, tunneling, entanglement, and environmental noise. From the Frequency Wave Theory perspective, investment keeps rising because governments and corporations are learning how to engineer reality at the frequency-and-phase level. Computation may be the public-facing justification, while the broader prize is programmable field control, producing new forms of sensing, communication, materials engineering, timing, energy manipulation, and eventually direct interaction with the deeper resonant structure of the vacuum.
